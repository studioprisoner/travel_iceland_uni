Explore the Northern Lights

The extreme dark of the Icelandic winter has a few advantages. Between September and April, Iceland is treated to a stunning natural display: the phenomenon of aurora borealis, or what we commonly call the Northern Lights.

From September to April, Iceland is a hotspot for this magnificent lightshow. The Aurora Borealis, more commonly referred to as the Northern Lights, is a natural occurrence created when solar particles interact with the atmosphere in the Earth's magnetic field. This releases energy, causing curious shimmering green streaks across the skies.

On clear winter nights, sightseeing trips are organized around this stunning natural spectacle. The best location for sightings varies and excursion leaders are competent in searching the lights, finding locations where environments are best for seeing them on any given night.

There are no assurances that you will see the Northern Lights during your stay, but in most instances, sightings are immediately improved outside populated areas, particularly away from the light-pollution of Reykjavik. Several hotels in the countryside offer special Northern Lights wake-up calls when they’re happening.

Photographing Northern Lights

Clear skies. If the weather cooperates, you are already half-way there. You can always check weather conditions, cloud coverage and Aurora activity on the Icelandic Met Office Website.

When it comes to equipment, the most crucial thing you can bring is a tripod and a cable release to avoid the dreaded shaken-photo-syndrome. If you don’t have a cable release, set your camera’s self-timer to two or ten seconds’ shutter delay.

There is no specific setting for your camera that ensures great photos, but if you have manual options, you are probably best served with experimenting with various combinations of ISO, aperture, and exposure settings. Generally, ISO setting between 800 and 3200, aperture between f/2.8 and f/5.6, and shutter speed at between 15 seconds and 30 seconds have proven useful.

Various combinations may give very different results. Higher ISO setting will allow you to capture faster exposures, but may also result in grainier images, for example. Note that shutter speeds of above 15 seconds will result in slight star movement. Wider angle lenses are typically more adaptable in low light settings, but longer lenses give you different options for compositions. You will probably get the best results with manual setting for infinite focal length.
